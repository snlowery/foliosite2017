Prysm

Visual workspaces where teams can create, edit and share

Prysm is a software suite designed to help enterprise customers communicate and work together in a shared interactive space. These spaces are stored in the cloud and available on any sized screen (from mobile to over 190 inches!).

Project Overview

The Challenge

In a multi-user collaborative environment, users expect to be aware of updates and important information. The project spaces had poor visibility of system status throughout the experience.

My Role

I was the interaction designer on this project, responsible for delivering wireframes and prototypes.

The Team

I worked with a UI designer, design manager, product owner, and a scrum team at Prysm. We also consulted members of our sales, training and customer support teams frequently.

Understanding The Problem

After kicking off the project, the team (plus members from sales, training, and support) met to map out our own assumptions and user pain points before moving forward.



Mapping Needs To Specific Personas

We were excited to break in our newly created proto personas. The design team worked closely with the product owner to map each user activity and the corresponding type of notifications that would be triggered. We used the personas to help us brainstorm and prioritize these ideas.

Ideation and Prototyping

What information do users need to see?

We quickly realized that there was a great deal of information we could communicate to our users, but how much was too much? We payed close attention to feedback we'd received from customers while creating our initial prototype.

Wireframes

Wireframes based on user stories for the project came together through a series of co-design sessions with the design team. We decided to group similar notifications in order to reduce visual clutter and minimize the amount of notifications a user would receive. We also thought it would be extremely useful to be able to take actions, like giving a user permission to enter the project, right from the notification. This meant that users had to be able to reach buttons on the notification, easier said than done on a 190' display!




Placement on a 190' screen

There were a number of factors to consider when placing notifications on the large display. First, users needed to be able to read their messages from 10 feet or 10 inches away. Secondly, they had to be able to physically reach the notification. Finally, notifications couldn't be too disruptive or cover central content on the screen. We tested many different solutions and found that placing notifications centrally at the bottom of the screen would meet all of our criteria.

Visual Design

With wireframes delivered, the UI designer got started on the visual design in sketch. The final designs were handed off to developers using a tool called Zeplin. Zeplin allows team members to post questions and comments right on the designs themselves.
